⛓️ Expandable Summary: Chemical Bonding

1. Ionic Bonding Overview
  • Defined as the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • Forms via the transfer of electrons from a metal (forms a positive cation) to a non-metal (forms a negative anion).
  • Results in a stable, repeating 3D structure called a Giant Ionic Lattice.
2. Representing Ionic Bonding
  • Dot-and-cross diagrams are used to show the transfer of outer-shell electrons.
  • The resulting ions are drawn in square brackets with the charge shown on the top-right.
  • The final compound must be electrically neutral, so the ratio of ions is determined by their charges (e.g., Ca²⁺ and 2x F⁻ to make CaF₂).
3. Ionic Trends
  • Cations (+) are always smaller than their parent atoms because they have lost an entire outer electron shell.
  • Anions (-) are always larger than their parent atoms due to increased electron-electron repulsion in the outer shell.
  • For isoelectronic ions (same electron configuration), the radius decreases as the proton number increases due to a stronger nuclear pull.
4. Physical Properties of Ionic Compounds
  • High Melting/Boiling Points: A large amount of energy is required to overcome the strong electrostatic forces within the giant lattice.
  • Electrical Conductivity: They do not conduct when solid (ions fixed), but do conduct when molten or dissolved (ions are mobile).
  • Solubility: Many are soluble in polar solvents like water, as the polar molecules can surround and stabilise the ions.
5. Covalent Bonding Overview
  • Defined as the strong electrostatic attraction between a shared pair of electrons and the nuclei of the two bonded atoms.
  • Occurs between non-metal atoms.
  • Can form single (2e⁻), double (4e⁻), or triple (6e⁻) bonds.
6. Covalent Dot-and-Cross Diagrams
  • Used to show shared electron pairs in overlapping outer shells.
  • Dative (coordinate) bonds occur when one atom donates both electrons to the shared pair. This is shown with an arrow (e.g., in NH₄⁺ and Al₂Cl₆).
  • Non-bonding electron pairs are called lone pairs.
7. Bond Length & Bond Strength
  • Bond Length: The distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms.
  • Bond Energy: The energy required to break the bond.
  • Generally, shorter bonds are stronger. The order of strength is: Triple > Double > Single. The order of length is: Single > Double > Triple.
8. Shapes of Covalent Compounds (VSEPR)
  • VSEPR Theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory states that electron pairs arrange themselves to minimise repulsion.
  • Order of Repulsion: Lone pair-lone pair > Lone pair-bond pair > Bond pair-bond pair.
  • Each lone pair reduces the bond angle by ~2.5°. For example, CH₄ (109.5°), NH₃ (107°), H₂O (104.5°).
