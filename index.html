• ⛓️ Chemical Bonding
    • 1. Ionic Bonding Overview
      • Definition: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
      • Formation: Electron transfer from a metal (cation) to a non-metal (anion).
      • Structure: Forms a Giant Ionic Lattice.
    • 2. Representing Ionic Bonding
      • Dot-and-Cross: Shows outer-shell e⁻ transfer. Ions are in [brackets] with charge.
      • Principle: Final compound must be electrically neutral.
    • 3. Ionic Trends
      • Ionic Radius: Cations (+) are smaller than atoms; Anions (-) are larger.
      • Isoelectronic Ions: Same e⁻ config. More protons = smaller radius.
    • 4. Physical Properties of Ionic Compounds
      • Melting/Boiling Point: High due to strong lattice forces.
      • Conductivity: Only when molten or aqueous (mobile ions).
      • Solubility: Often soluble in polar solvents (e.g., H₂O).
    • 5. Covalent Bonding Overview
      • Definition: Electrostatic attraction between shared e⁻ pair and two nuclei.
      • Formation: Electron sharing between non-metals.
      • Types: Single, Double, Triple.
    • 6. Covalent Dot-and-Cross Diagrams
      • Shows shared e⁻ pairs in overlapping shells. Also shows lone pairs.
      • Dative Bonding: One atom donates both e⁻. Represented by an arrow (→). E.g., NH₄⁺.
    • 7. Bond Length & Strength
      • Relationship: Shorter bonds are stronger.
      • Length Order: Single > Double > Triple
      • Strength Order: Triple > Double > Single
    • 8. Shapes of Covalent Compounds
      • VSEPR Theory: Electron pairs repel to minimize repulsion.
      • Repulsion Order: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP
      • Key Shapes & Angles
        • Linear: 180° (2 BP)
        • Trigonal Planar: 120° (3 BP)
        • Tetrahedral: 109.5° (4 BP)
        • Trigonal Pyramidal: 107° (3 BP, 1 LP)
        • Bent/Non-linear: 104.5° (2 BP, 2 LP)
        • Octahedral: 90° (6 BP)
